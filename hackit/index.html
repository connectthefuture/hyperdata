Background

I really enjoyed myself making Get Your Data Out! back in 2008 (how time flies) and recently wanted to apply myself to some fun projects to keep the black dog at bay. So I've made another geek-pop advocacy video called:

Can I Hack It?

a little celebration of Open Source

It's a public information broadcast, just under 4 minutes long, briefly describing what open source is, why it should be encouraged, an overview of the best-known licenses and introducing a bunch of open source applications. With a bouncy soundtrack to help stop you getting bored.

All the source and media files I used are here. Please feel free to rebroadcast, remix, etc.

I personally believe Open Source is very important and anything that encourages it should in turn be encouraged, so I'll be grateful for any links on Facebook, Twitter, Google Plus etc.

How it was Made

Audio

I'm afraid the audio wasn't recorded on open source software. For historical reasons* I used the EnergyXT (€59) on MS Windows. It's a fairly standard multitrack recording application, similar to those mentioned below. (*I had soundcard issues when I first set up my audio workstation, to save time I installed MS Windows and used the manufacturer's drivers). But I also used various (free) VST plugins for processing with EnergyXT as host, plus Audacity for preprocessing samples.

The audio hardware included a fairly standard (Frankenstein) desktop PC with Delta 66 soundcard (4-channel input, 4-channel output, €195), plus a tambourine and all the other bits and pieces in my home studio.

For mixdown I usually play stuff into a little Zoom H2 recorder. It doesn't seem to impact the quality perceptibly, and it's very convenient, I just send it what would be going to the speakers from the mixer.

Final tweaks and conversion to mp3 I did with the essential Audacity (with lame installed).

Video

The visuals were put together using entirely open source software on my laptop running Ubuntu.

Although the idea is simple enough, it took me a long time to find the tools that I needed. Video formats are a total nightmare, finding an approach that would produce something that kept the sync and was suitable for YouTube took ages.

Anyhow, it's basically a slide show presentation, so I started with LibreOffice Impress. To hold the same slide over several bars of music it's simply repeated in the slide deck. The only problem with that is I needed 500+ slides. Lots of copy & paste (and I'm afraid LibreOffice gets a little bit crashy with that many slides).

Quite a lot of Web searching was also involved to find all the logos etc, but I don't like to moan... Once the slides were assembled I did export as html, which actually produces an image for each slide plus some minimal HTML to tie them together (the XHTML export has the slide text as content). I exported 800x600 PNGs, an option for which I think I needed to install docvert.

From there I did a bunch of pre-processing using quick & dirty scripts:

  • process.sh - glue for the other scripts (this shows the sequence of operations)
  • rename.py - renames the image files so they'll load in correct order
  • resize.sh - resizes the images to 640x480

After a lot of failed attempts with combinations of imagemagick, mencoder and ffmpeg etc. on the command-line, I managed to get the desired result using LiVES, a powerful video editor/VJ tool.

Also useful was mediainfo, which will tell you the format details of an existing media file.

The music is 140bpm, so I used an initial frame rate of 2.3333333 fps, with slides I wanted to show for more than one beat simply repeated. Afterwards I changed the fps in LiVES to 25fps, added the soundtrack and exported.

Of course I had to repeat this procedure a lot of times to get the sync right and correct mistakes. But I don't like to moan.

Amaya was used for authoring this document.
